Introduction — Why Capacity Planning Matters

▼

Running out of disk space on a production server is not a minor inconvenience — it is an outage. Web servers stop serving requests, databases refuse to write transactions, and log daemons silently drop events the moment a filesystem hits 100%. Capacity planning is the discipline that prevents this by anticipating growth before it becomes a crisis.

Capacity Planning Methodology

▼

Good capacity planning follows three sequential steps: measure what you have, estimate how fast it grows, and add headroom so you never hit the wall. Skip any step and your plan is a guess.

Step 1 — Establish a Current Usage Baseline

You cannot plan for growth if you do not know your starting point. Capture current disk usage on every filesystem that matters.

Linux — df -h

The df command reports disk space usage for all mounted filesystems. The -h flag formats sizes in human-readable units (GB, MB).

# Show all mounted filesystems in human-readable units
df -h

# Example output:
Filesystem      Size  Used Avail Use% Mounted on
/dev/sda1        50G   18G   29G  38% /
/dev/sdb1       200G  142G   49G  74% /var/log
tmpfs           3.9G     0  3.9G   0% /dev/shm

Pay close attention to the Use% column. Any filesystem above 70% warrants a growth projection. Above 85% requires immediate action.

Windows — Get-PSDrive

In PowerShell, Get-PSDrive lists all drives including their used and free space. Filter for filesystem drives with -PSProvider FileSystem.

# List all filesystem drives with used/free space
Get-PSDrive -PSProvider FileSystem |
  Select-Object Name, Used, Free |
  Format-Table -AutoSize

# For GB values, divide by 1GB:
Get-PSDrive -PSProvider FileSystem |
  Select-Object Name,
    @{N='Used_GB';E={[math]::Round($_.Used/1GB,2)}},
    @{N='Free_GB';E={[math]::Round($_.Free/1GB,2)}} |
  Format-Table -AutoSize
✅ Best Practice — Automate Baseline Collection
Run these commands on a schedule (cron job / Task Scheduler) and log the output to a file. A time-series record of disk usage is the raw data you need to calculate an accurate growth rate in Step 2.

Step 2 — Estimate the Growth Rate

Growth rate (r) is the percentage by which storage consumption increases per unit of time (typically per year). There are two ways to determine it.

Method A — Measured Growth Rate (preferred)

If you have baseline data from two points in time, calculate the annualized growth rate directly:

r = (C_now − C_then) ÷ C_then
where C_now and C_then are usage at two points separated by one year (or scaled proportionally)

Example: Six months ago a log volume was 80 GB; today it is 104 GB. Growth over 6 months = 30%. Annualized: 30% × 2 = 60%/year. (Use this rate cautiously — log growth can be bursty.)

Method B — Industry Benchmarks (when no history exists)

Environment Type Typical Annual Growth Rate Notes
General-purpose file server 15–25% Stable user base, moderate document growth
Web application server 20–40% Depends heavily on traffic growth
Centralized log storage (Splunk) 30–80% Grows as more log sources are onboarded
Database server 20–50% Driven by transaction volume and retention policies
Backup target 20–30% Mirrors growth of source data
Lab / development VM 10–20% Relatively stable; periodic cleanups help
💡 Tip — Conservative Planning
When in doubt, use the upper end of the benchmark range. Storage is cheaper than an outage. If your measured growth rate is 25% but the benchmark says up to 40%, plan for 40%.

Step 3 — Add Headroom

Even a perfectly accurate growth projection is not enough. Filesystems degrade in performance and reliability as they approach capacity, and unexpected spikes happen. The industry standard is to maintain 20–30% free space at all times.

Why Headroom Matters

  • Filesystem performance: Many filesystems (ext4, XFS, NTFS) slow down significantly above 80–85% capacity due to fragmentation and metadata overhead.
  • Log spikes: A security incident or application error storm can generate 10× normal log volume in minutes.
  • Temporary files: Package updates, database vacuums, and backup staging all require temporary disk space.
  • Splunk indexing overhead: Splunk writes bloom filters, metadata, and journal files alongside indexed data — these consume space beyond the raw event data.

The Headroom Multiplier

In the capacity planning formula (Section 3), we apply a 1.25× multiplier to the projected data volume. This reserves 20% of total provisioned capacity as free headroom — the minimum recommended buffer. For high-volatility environments like centralized log stores, consider 1.33× (25% free headroom).

⚠ Warning — The 85% Rule
Set an alert when any filesystem reaches 85% full. That gives you time to act before hitting 100%. At 95% full, many systems begin experiencing write failures. At 100%, services crash. Never let a production filesystem reach 100%.

The Capacity Planning Formula

▼

The standard capacity planning formula combines your current usage, an estimated annual growth rate, a planning horizon, and a headroom buffer into a single projected storage requirement.

C_planned = C_current × (1 + r)^t × 1.25
C_current = current used storage  |  r = annual growth rate (decimal)  |  t = planning horizon in years  |  1.25 = 20% headroom multiplier

Variable Reference

VariableMeaningExample Value
C_currentCurrent used storage (GB or TB)500 GB
rAnnual growth rate as a decimal0.25 (= 25%/year)
tPlanning horizon in years2 (= 2 years)
(1 + r)^tCompound growth factor(1.25)² = 1.5625
1.25Headroom multiplier (20% free space buffer)Fixed
C_plannedTotal storage to provisionResult in GB or TB

Worked Example 1 — File Server, Moderate Growth

Scenario: A departmental file server currently uses 800 GB. Historical data shows 20% annual growth. Plan for 3 years.

  • Identify inputs: C_current = 800 GB, r = 0.20, t = 3
  • Calculate the compound growth factor: (1 + 0.20)³ = (1.20)³ = 1.728
  • Project data volume at year 3: 800 × 1.728 = 1,382.4 GB
  • Apply the 1.25× headroom multiplier: 1,382.4 × 1.25 = 1,728 GB
  • Round up to a practical provisioning size: 2 TB (next standard drive/volume size above 1,728 GB)
✅ Provision at least 2 TB to support this file server for 3 years with adequate headroom.

Worked Example 2 — Log Storage, Rapid Growth

Scenario: A centralized syslog server currently holds 120 GB of logs. New systems are being onboarded rapidly; growth rate is estimated at 60%/year. Plan for 18 months (1.5 years).

  • Identify inputs: C_current = 120 GB, r = 0.60, t = 1.5
  • Calculate the compound growth factor: (1 + 0.60)^1.5 = (1.60)^1.5 ≈ 2.024
  • Project data volume at 18 months: 120 × 2.024 = 242.9 GB
  • Apply the 1.25× headroom multiplier: 242.9 × 1.25 = 303.6 GB
  • Round up: 350–400 GB is a practical provisioning target
✅ Provision at least 350 GB to sustain this log server for 18 months at 60% annual growth.

Storage Tiering — Hot, Warm, and Cold

▼

Not all data needs the same speed of storage — and not all storage costs the same per gigabyte. Storage tiering is the practice of matching data to the most cost-effective storage tier based on how frequently that data is accessed. This is especially important for log data, which is heavily accessed when new but rarely touched after 30–90 days.

The Three Storage Tiers

🔥 HOT
NVMe SSD / Enterprise SSD Actively accessed data — current logs (last 7–30 days), live databases, OS and application binaries. Requires maximum IOPS and minimum latency. Data is read and written frequently.
~$100–250/TB
Fastest · Most expensive
↓ Data ages → access frequency drops → move to warm tier
🌡 WARM
SATA SSD / 7,200 RPM HDD RAID Infrequently accessed data — logs from 30–90 days ago, completed project archives, monthly reports. Accessed occasionally for investigations or audits. Moderate throughput acceptable.
~$30–100/TB
Balanced · Moderate cost
↓ Data ages further → rarely accessed → move to cold tier
🧊 COLD
High-capacity HDD / Tape / Object Storage Archival data — logs older than 90 days, compliance archives, annual backups. Accessed very rarely (perhaps for a legal hold or annual audit). Retrieval latency of minutes to hours is acceptable.
~$5–20/TB
Slowest · Cheapest

When to Move Data Between Tiers

Trigger Move From Move To Example
Data age exceeds policy threshold Hot Warm Splunk moves logs older than 30 days from hot to warm buckets automatically
Hot tier reaches 80% capacity Hot Warm Oldest hot-tier data migrated to free space for new ingestion
Retention policy (e.g., 90-day active window) Warm Cold Compliance requires 7-year retention; data beyond 90 days moves to cold archive
Retention period expires Cold Deleted Logs older than 7 years are purged per data retention policy

Splunk and Storage Tiers

Splunk Enterprise manages tiering automatically through its bucket lifecycle. Indexes are divided into buckets that progress through stages: hot → warm → cold → frozen. You configure the age thresholds and storage paths for each stage in the index configuration. For your Project 3 lab deployment on Splunk Free, you will work with a simplified single-tier setup — but understanding the tier model helps you interpret Splunk's storage configuration options and plan disk allocation.

💡 Lab Reality Check
In a production Splunk deployment, hot and warm indexes typically live on SSDs while cold indexes move to cheaper HDD storage. In your Project 3 lab VM, you will likely have a single disk — which means everything is effectively on one tier. The tier model still matters for sizing: you need to know your total retention window and estimate total volume accordingly.

Log Retention & Splunk Storage Sizing

▼

Why Log Data Grows

Log volume is driven by the number of events generated per second across all monitored systems. Every user login, firewall connection, web request, service restart, and cron job produces log entries. As you add more monitored hosts, enable more log sources, or increase system activity, daily ingestion volume grows — often non-linearly.

Estimating Daily Log Volume Per Host Type

These are typical raw (uncompressed) daily log volumes for common server types in a small-to-medium environment. Actual volumes vary significantly based on activity level and verbosity settings.

Host Type Typical Daily Raw Volume Primary Log Sources Notes
Linux server (light activity) 10–30 MB/day syslog, auth.log, cron Low-traffic server; minimal services
Linux server (moderate activity) 30–100 MB/day syslog, auth.log, application logs Web server, app server with real traffic
Windows Server (light) 20–50 MB/day Security, System, Application event logs Few users, minimal audit policy
Windows Server (with AD/auditing) 50–200 MB/day Security event log (logon events, object access) Verbose audit policy significantly increases volume
pfSense / firewall 20–80 MB/day firewall rules, DHCP, VPN, IDS alerts Scales with network traffic volume
High-traffic web server 100 MB – 1 GB/day Access logs, error logs Access log volume is directly proportional to request rate

Splunk Raw vs. Indexed Data Sizing

When Splunk ingests raw log data, it does not store it as-is. Splunk compresses and indexes the data, creating additional metadata structures (bloom filters, lexicons, tsidx files) alongside the compressed raw events. The net effect on disk space depends on the data type:

Data Type Typical Compression Indexed Size vs. Raw Notes
Syslog / plain text 50–70% 30–50% of raw size Text compresses very well
Windows Event Log (XML) 60–75% 25–40% of raw size XML is verbose; compresses extremely well
JSON-formatted logs 40–60% 40–60% of raw size Structured but less repetitive than XML
Binary / already-compressed 0–15% 85–100%+ of raw size Avoid sending pre-compressed data to Splunk
📐 Splunk Sizing Rule of Thumb
For planning purposes, use a 30% compression ratio as a conservative estimate for mixed log data. This means:

Indexed size ≈ Raw daily volume × 0.70

For a 90-day retention window: Total indexed storage = Raw daily volume × 0.70 × 90

Then apply the 1.25× headroom multiplier for Splunk's metadata overhead and growth buffer.

The Splunk Free 500 MB/Day Ingestion Limit

Splunk Free (the license tier used in this course) caps daily data ingestion at 500 MB of raw log data per day. This has direct implications for your Project 3 lab deployment:

  • If your combined daily log volume from all forwarding hosts exceeds 500 MB, Splunk Free will stop indexing new data for the remainder of that calendar day.
  • For a small lab environment (3–6 servers, moderate activity), 500 MB/day is generally sufficient — typical combined volume is 50–200 MB/day.
  • The limit applies to raw ingestion volume, not indexed size.
  • Splunk Enterprise (licensed) removes this cap; Splunk Cloud has separate pricing tiers.
⚠ Project 3 Implication
For Project 3, you will be forwarding logs from Linux servers, a Windows server, and a pfSense firewall. Estimate your combined daily volume before you configure forwarding to confirm you will stay under the 500 MB/day limit. The Mini-Case in Section 7 walks through exactly this calculation for a representative lab environment.

SPL Reinforcement Exercise — Observing Log Volume

▼
✅ Ungraded Activity — ~20 Minutes
This exercise uses the shared Splunk instance from Week 9. It is not graded. Its purpose is to connect the storage sizing concepts you just learned to real observed data, and to keep your SPL skills fresh before Project 3. No new SPL commands are introduced here.

Log in to the shared Splunk instance used in Week 9. Run each of the two searches below, observe the results, and record your findings in the text areas provided. You will then use your observed daily event count to estimate 90-day storage requirements using the capacity planning formula.

🔍 Search 1 — Event Volume Over Time

This search creates a time-based chart of event counts in the network index, bucketed by hour. It shows you the daily rhythm of log activity and any spikes.

index=network | timechart count span=1h

What to look for: Identify the peak hourly event count and the approximate total events over a 24-hour period. Note any obvious spikes that might indicate a scan, error storm, or scheduled job.

🔍 Search 2 — Event Count by Source Type

This search breaks down total event counts by sourcetype, showing you which log sources are generating the most data. This is essential for identifying your highest-volume contributors before sizing storage.

index=network | stats count by sourcetype

What to look for: Which sourcetype has the highest event count? Is the distribution roughly even, or does one source dominate? A single high-volume source (e.g., firewall connection logs) can drive the majority of your storage requirement.

🧮 Apply the Formula to Your Observed Data

Using your observed daily event count from Search 1, estimate the 90-day indexed storage requirement. Assume an average event size of 500 bytes and a 30% compression ratio (indexed size = 70% of raw).

📐 Calculation Steps
  1. Daily raw volume (GB) = (Daily event count × 500 bytes) ÷ 1,073,741,824
  2. Daily indexed volume (GB) = Daily raw volume × 0.70
  3. 90-day indexed volume (GB) = Daily indexed volume × 90
  4. With 1.25× headroom = 90-day indexed volume × 1.25
🔗 Connection to Project 3
The searches above are the same type of volume-assessment queries you will run on your own Splunk deployment in Project 3 to verify that log ingestion is working and to monitor daily volume against the 500 MB/day Splunk Free limit. Getting comfortable with timechart and stats count by sourcetype now pays off directly when you are troubleshooting your Project 3 deployment.

Mini-Case — How Much Storage Does Splunk Need?

▼

Work through this step-by-step sizing calculation for a representative small-business Splunk deployment. This environment closely mirrors what you will build in Project 3. Click Reveal Answer after each step to check your work.

📋 Environment Specification
  • 3 Linux servers — estimated 25 MB/day raw logs each
  • 2 Windows servers — estimated 35 MB/day raw logs each
  • 1 pfSense firewall — estimated 20 MB/day raw logs
  • Retention window: 90 days
  • Compression ratio: 30% (indexed = 70% of raw)
  • Headroom multiplier: 1.25×
Step 1 — Calculate Total Daily Raw Log Volume

Add up the raw daily log volume from all six sources.

(3 Linux × 25 MB) + (2 Windows × 35 MB) + (1 pfSense × 20 MB) = 75 + 70 + 20 = 165 MB/day raw

✅ 165 MB/day is well below the 500 MB/day Splunk Free limit. This environment is a safe fit for Splunk Free.

Step 2 — Calculate Daily Indexed Volume (30% compression)

Apply the compression ratio to find how much disk space Splunk will actually use per day.

165 MB × 0.70 = 115.5 MB/day indexed

The 30% compression saves approximately 49.5 MB per day compared to storing raw logs. Over 90 days, that adds up to ~4.5 GB saved.

Step 3 — Calculate 90-Day Indexed Storage Requirement

Multiply the daily indexed volume by the 90-day retention window.

115.5 MB/day × 90 days = 10,395 MB ≈ 10.2 GB indexed
Step 4 — Add 1.25× Headroom Multiplier

Apply the headroom multiplier to account for Splunk metadata, bloom filters, and growth buffer.

10.2 GB × 1.25 = 12.75 GB total provisioned storage

Round up to 15–20 GB as a practical provisioning target for your Splunk index volume. This is easily accommodated on a standard lab VM with a 50–100 GB virtual disk.

Step 5 — Verify Against Splunk Free 500 MB/Day Limit

Confirm that the total daily raw ingestion volume stays within the Splunk Free license constraint.

✅ Daily raw volume = 165 MB/day  |  Splunk Free limit = 500 MB/day
165 MB < 500 MB — Within limit with 335 MB/day of headroom

This environment has significant room to grow before hitting the Splunk Free cap. You could add approximately 2 more moderately active servers (at ~50 MB/day each) before approaching the limit.

✅ Mini-Case Summary
For a 6-host environment generating 165 MB/day of raw logs with a 90-day retention policy and 30% compression:
  • Daily raw ingestion: 165 MB (well within Splunk Free 500 MB/day limit)
  • Daily indexed storage: ~115.5 MB
  • 90-day indexed storage: ~10.2 GB
  • Recommended provisioned storage (with headroom): 15–20 GB
A lab VM with a 50 GB virtual disk is more than adequate for this deployment.
