1. What Is Disaster Recovery?

Disaster Recovery (DR) is the set of policies, tools, and procedures designed to enable the recovery or continuation of vital technology infrastructure and systems following a natural or human-caused disaster.

DR focuses specifically on restoring IT systems and data after an outage. It is a subset of a broader discipline called Business Continuity (BC), which addresses keeping all business functions operational—not just technology.

Business Continuity vs. Disaster Recovery

DimensionBusiness Continuity (BC)Disaster Recovery (DR)
ScopeEntire organization (people, facilities, supply chain, IT)IT infrastructure and data
FocusMaintaining critical business functions during and after disruptionRestoring IT systems and data to operational state

ExamplesAlternate work sites, communication plans, manual workaroundsBackup restoration, failover to secondary site, system rebuild

Key Takeaway: Every organization—regardless of size—needs a disaster recovery plan. The question is never if a disaster will occur, but when. The infrastructure you built in this course (servers, directory services, firewalls, monitoring) represents significant investment. DR planning protects that investment.

2. Types of Disasters

Disasters that threaten IT infrastructure fall into three broad categories. Effective DR planning requires considering threats from all categories, not just the most dramatic ones.

Important distinction from Week 10: RAID protects against a single disk failure (fault tolerance), but it is not a backup. RAID cannot protect against accidental deletion, ransomware, fire, or multi-disk failure. Disaster recovery requires actual backups stored separately from the production system.

3. Recovery Time Objective (RTO) & Recovery Point Objective (RPO)

RTO and RPO are the two most critical metrics in disaster recovery planning. Together they define how quickly you must recover and how much data loss is acceptable.

Recovery Time Objective (RTO)

Definition: The maximum tolerable duration of time that a system can be down after a failure or disaster before the impact becomes unacceptable to the organization.

In plain terms: "How long can we be without this system before it's a serious problem?"

Recovery Point Objective (RPO)

Definition: The maximum tolerable period of time in which data might be lost due to a disaster, measured backward from the moment of the disaster to the last usable backup.

In plain terms: "How much data (measured in time) can we afford to lose?"

RTO & RPO Timeline

RTO & RPO at Different Scales

Example RTO and RPO values for different system types

The Cost Relationship: Tighter RTO and RPO requirements cost more to achieve. A near-zero RPO may require real-time replication (expensive). A 24-hour RPO only requires daily backups (inexpensive). DR planning always involves balancing protection against budget.

4. Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

A Business Impact Analysis is the process of identifying an organization's critical systems and determining the impact of their unavailability. The BIA drives RTO and RPO decisions by answering: "What happens to the business if this system goes down, and for how long?"

BIA Process

  1. Inventory systems – List all IT systems and services.
  2. Assess impact – For each system, determine the business impact of downtime (financial loss, productivity loss, legal/compliance risk, reputation damage).
  3. Assign criticality – Rate each system (Critical, High, Medium, Low).
  4. Determine RTO/RPO – Based on criticality and impact, assign recovery targets.
  5. Prioritize recovery order – Systems with the lowest RTO are recovered first.

Notice the pattern: The pfSense firewall has a very tight RTO (1 hour) because without network connectivity, nothing else works—but its RPO is relaxed (1 week) because its configuration rarely changes. Conversely, an email server might have a tight RPO (4 hours) because messages are constantly arriving, even if its RTO is more relaxed.
Deeper Dive: Quantifying Business Impact

Organizations often quantify impact in dollar terms to justify DR spending:

  • Revenue impact: Lost sales per hour of downtime (e.g., an e-commerce site generating $10,000/hour).
  • Productivity impact: Number of employees idle × average hourly cost.
  • Contractual impact: SLA penalties, late delivery fees.
  • Regulatory impact: Fines for non-compliance (HIPAA, PCI-DSS).
  • Reputation impact: Customer trust erosion (hardest to quantify).

For your Project 3 lab environment, think of criticality in terms of dependency chains: if pfSense is down, no other system can communicate externally. If AD is down, no one can authenticate. These dependencies determine recovery priority.

5. DR Plan Components & Testing

A complete disaster recovery plan addresses three pillars: People, Processes, and Technology. The plan must be documented, accessible, and regularly tested.

People – Roles & Communication
  • DR team roles: DR coordinator, system administrators (by platform), network administrator, management liaison.
  • Contact lists: Primary and secondary contacts with phone, email, and alternate communication methods.
  • Escalation procedures: Who to contact if the primary person is unavailable; when to escalate to management or vendors.
  • Communication plan: How to notify stakeholders (employees, customers, partners) during an incident.
Processes – Recovery Procedures
  • Recovery procedures (runbooks): Step-by-step instructions for restoring each system, written so that someone other than the original administrator can follow them.
  • Recovery priority order: Which systems to restore first (based on BIA).
  • Decision trees: Criteria for declaring a disaster; when to fail over vs. repair in place.
  • Communication procedures: Status update cadence, stakeholder notification templates.
  • Post-incident review: After-action report template, lessons learned process.
Technology – Backup & Recovery Infrastructure
  • Backup systems: Automated backup solutions (covered in Module 15.3), backup storage hardware/services.
  • Recovery sites: Hot site (ready to go), warm site (partially configured), cold site (space and power only)—awareness level.
  • Redundant infrastructure: Redundant network paths, UPS/generator, spare hardware.
  • Recovery tools: Boot media, installation media, recovery keys, license keys—all stored securely offsite.
Documentation Requirements

A DR plan is only useful if it is:

  • Written down – Not just in someone's head.
  • Accessible during a disaster – Stored offsite/cloud, not only on the servers it's meant to recover.
  • Current – Updated whenever infrastructure changes.
  • Clear – Written for the audience who will use it under stress.

Minimum documentation includes: system inventory, BIA table, contact list, recovery priority matrix, step-by-step runbooks, backup schedule and locations, and verification/testing logs.

Testing Cadence – Validating the Plan

An untested plan is only slightly better than no plan. Testing levels (from least to most disruptive):

Test TypeDescriptionFrequency
Documentation reviewWalk through the plan on paper; verify contacts, procedures, and assumptions are current.Quarterly
Tabletop exerciseTeam discusses a hypothetical scenario step-by-step; identifies gaps without touching systems.Semi-annually
Partial testRestore a single system or subset from backup; verify data integrity and measure recovery time.Monthly–Quarterly
Full DR drillSimulate complete failure; recover all critical systems; measure actual RTO against targets.Annually

Key principle: Every test should produce a written report documenting what worked, what failed, and what changes are needed. The DR plan is a living document.

6. Mini-Case: Assigning RTO & RPO

Scenario: Riverside Community Library

Riverside Community Library serves 5,000 patrons and has a small IT team of two people. They operate the following systems:

  • Catalog Server – Hosts the online catalog that patrons and staff use to search and check out books. Used constantly during operating hours (9 AM–8 PM).
  • Public Wi-Fi Gateway – Provides internet access to ~200 daily visitors. Patrons expect it to be available whenever the library is open.
  • Staff File Server – Stores administrative documents, grant applications, and financial records. Staff access it throughout the day.
  • Digital Archive Server – Hosts a digitized collection of local historical documents. Accessed occasionally by researchers; content changes rarely (new items added monthly).

The library's annual IT budget is modest. They cannot afford real-time replication or hot-site solutions. Consider what RTO and RPO would be reasonable for each system given the library's size, budget, and usage patterns.

Question 1: Catalog Server – RTO

What is the most appropriate RTO for the Catalog Server?

Correct! 2–4 hours is appropriate. The catalog is critical during operating hours (staff cannot check out books without it), so extended downtime is unacceptable. However, 15 minutes would require expensive high-availability solutions beyond the library's budget. A few hours allows the team to restore from backup or troubleshoot while staff use manual workarounds temporarily.

Question 2: Catalog Server – RPO

What is the most appropriate RPO for the Catalog Server?

Correct! 24 hours (daily backup) is reasonable. The catalog changes throughout the day as books are checked in/out, but these transactions can be re-entered from physical records if needed. A daily backup balances data protection against the library's limited budget. Real-time replication would be overkill for this use case.

Question 3: Digital Archive Server – RTO

What is the most appropriate RTO for the Digital Archive Server?

Correct! 1–3 days is appropriate. The archive is accessed occasionally by researchers—not daily by all staff. Its unavailability is an inconvenience, not a crisis. The small IT team can prioritize more critical systems first and restore the archive when time permits.

Question 4: Digital Archive Server – RPO

What is the most appropriate RPO for the Digital Archive Server?

Correct! A monthly RPO (or even weekly) is reasonable here. New items are only added monthly, so the data changes very infrequently. A monthly full backup captures all changes with minimal risk of significant data loss. This also minimizes storage costs for what is likely a large collection of image files.

Question 5: Recovery Priority Order

Based on the scenario, which system should be restored first after a disaster that takes down all four systems simultaneously?

Correct! The Public Wi-Fi Gateway should be restored first. Like a firewall, the gateway provides fundamental network connectivity. Without it, patrons have no internet access and other network-dependent systems (including the catalog) may not function properly. Restoring network infrastructure first enables everything else to come back online. This mirrors the pattern in enterprise environments where network/firewall devices have the tightest RTO.
