4.1 Network Interface Cards (NICs)

Network Interface Cards connect servers to networks and are critical for data transfer performance. Unlike desktop systems that typically use 1 Gigabit Ethernet, servers often require higher bandwidth to handle multiple simultaneous connections, virtual machines, and storage traffic.

Server Networking Requirements

Server NICs prioritize different characteristics than consumer network cards. Key differences include support for higher speeds (10/25/100 GbE), hardware offload capabilities (TCP/IP processing), multiple ports for redundancy, and SR-IOV for virtual machine networking. Enterprise NICs also support advanced features like RDMA (Remote Direct Memory Access) for ultra-low latency storage networks.

4.2 Power Supply Units (PSUs)

Power supply units convert AC power from the wall outlet to DC power used by server components. PSU efficiency directly impacts electricity costs and heat generation, making it a critical consideration for 24/7 server operation.

Why PSU Efficiency Matters

An inefficient PSU wastes electricity as heat. For example, an 80% efficient PSU powering a 400W server draws 500W from the wall (400W useful + 100W wasted as heat). A 90% efficient PSU only draws 444W for the same load (400W useful + 44W wasted). Over one year of continuous operation, this difference can save hundreds of dollars in electricity costs and reduce cooling requirements.

2. A mission-critical database server consumes 400W continuously (24/7). Electricity costs $0.12/kWh. What is the approximate annual savings when using a Gold (90%) PSU versus 80 Plus (80%) PSU?

4.3 Data Center Cooling Systems

Server cooling is essential for reliability and performance. Every watt of power consumed by IT equipment generates heat that must be removed. Inefficient cooling can consume 50% or more of total data center power, making cooling design critical for both equipment protection and operating cost control.

The Heat Problem

Modern servers generate enormous amounts of heat in small spaces. A typical rack of servers can produce 5-15 kW of heat (equivalent to running 50-150 hair dryers continuously). Without adequate cooling, component temperatures rise rapidly, leading to thermal throttling (reduced performance), hardware failures, and shortened equipment lifespan. Proper cooling design ensures stable temperatures while minimizing energy consumption.

Hot Aisle / Cold Aisle Design

The hot aisle / cold aisle layout is the industry standard for efficient data center cooling. By organizing server racks in alternating rows, this design separates cold supply air from hot exhaust air, improving cooling efficiency by 20-30%.

Benefits of Hot Aisle / Cold Aisle Design

  • 20-30% improvement in cooling efficiency
  • Eliminates hot spots and temperature variations
  • Reduces cooling system energy costs
  • Improves equipment reliability and lifespan
  • Enables higher rack densities
  • Better predictability for capacity planning

Containment Systems: Advanced implementations add doors, panels, or curtains to completely contain either the hot or cold aisles. This prevents mixing of hot and cold air, further improving efficiency. Cold aisle containment (CAC) and hot aisle containment (HAC) can improve cooling efficiency by an additional 10-20% beyond basic hot/cold aisle layout.

Cooling Capacity Planning

Proper cooling capacity ensures equipment operates within safe temperature ranges. The fundamental principle is that all electrical power consumed eventually becomes heat that must be removed.

Calculation Formula: Watts √ó 3.412 = BTU/hour. One watt of electrical power produces 3.412 BTU/hour of heat. We add 20% overhead to account for cooling system inefficiencies, ambient heat gain, and future growth capacity.

Additional Cooling Considerations

  • Ambient Temperature: Hot climates require 10-15% additional cooling capacity
  • Altitude: Higher elevations (above 3,000 feet) reduce cooling efficiency by ~5% per 1,000 feet
  • Redundancy: Critical facilities typically deploy N+1 or 2N cooling redundancy (doubling capacity requirements)
  • Humidity Control: Data centers maintain 40-60% relative humidity to prevent static electricity and condensation
  • Airflow Management: Blanking panels, cable management, and proper rack layout prevent air recirculation and hot spots

Knowledge Check: Cooling Systems

3. A data center has 15 servers consuming 400W each. Using the formula (Watts √ó 3.412 = BTU/hr) with 20% overhead, what cooling capacity is required?

Correct! Calculation: 15 servers √ó 400W = 6,000W total. 6,000W √ó 3.412 = 20,472 BTU/hr base heat generation. With 20% overhead: 20,472 √ó 1.2 = 24,566 BTU/hr. This equals approximately 2 tons of cooling (12,000 BTU/hr per ton). Most installations would specify a 24,000 BTU/hr (2-ton) unit as the nearest standard capacity.

4. Which NIC bonding mode provides both increased bandwidth AND automatic failover?

Correct! Active-Active bonding (also called LACP - Link Aggregation Control Protocol / Mode 4) distributes traffic across multiple NICs for increased bandwidth while providing automatic failover if one NIC fails. Active-Passive provides failover but no bandwidth increase, while single NIC provides neither benefit.

5. A server requires 600W continuous power. For N+1 redundancy with 90% efficient PSUs, what capacity should each PSU have?

Correct! For N+1, each PSU must handle the full server load independently. 600W server load √∑ 0.90 efficiency = 667W minimum capacity. Rounding up to the next standard PSU size gives 750W. This ensures each PSU can power the entire server if the other fails, with some headroom for peak loads.

6. What is the primary benefit of hot aisle / cold aisle design in data centers?

Correct! Hot/cold aisle design separates cold supply air (cold aisle) from hot exhaust air (hot aisle), preventing the two from mixing. This allows cooling systems to operate more efficiently, typically improving efficiency by 20-30% and reducing energy costs. It also eliminates hot spots and provides more predictable cooling performance.

  • N+1 redundancy ensures zero downtime from PSU failure by providing one spare PSU that can handle full server load
  • All electrical power becomes heat (Watts √ó 3.412 = BTU/hr) that must be removed by cooling systems
  • Hot aisle / cold aisle design improves cooling efficiency 20-30% by preventing hot and cold air mixing
  • Proper infrastructure planning (networking, power, cooling) is essential for reliable 24/7 server operation
