What Is Cloud Computing?

In Modules 13.1–13.3, you learned how virtualization decouples operating systems from physical hardware using hypervisors. Cloud computing takes that same abstraction and delivers it as a service over a network—usually the internet. Instead of buying, racking, and managing your own servers, you rent computing resources from a provider who handles the underlying infrastructure at massive scale.

📖 NIST SP 800-145 Definition

"Cloud computing is a model for enabling ubiquitous, convenient, on-demand network access to a shared pool of configurable computing resources (e.g., networks, servers, storage, applications, and services) that can be rapidly provisioned and released with minimal management effort or service provider interaction."

— National Institute of Standards and Technology, SP 800-145 (September 2011)

The Five Essential Characteristics

NIST identifies five characteristics that a service must exhibit to qualify as cloud computing. Expand each one below to see a definition and a practical example.

Definition: A consumer can provision computing resources—such as VMs, storage, or databases—automatically, without requiring human interaction with the service provider.

Example: A developer logs into the AWS Management Console at 2 AM and spins up three new EC2 instances for load testing. No phone call, no ticket—just a few clicks and the resources are live within minutes.

Connection to your labs: Think about how you create a VM in VirtualBox. Cloud self-service is the same idea, but the hypervisor is in someone else's data center and you access it through a web portal or CLI.

Definition: Cloud resources are available over the network and accessed through standard mechanisms (web browsers, APIs, mobile apps) from a wide variety of client devices.

Example: You can manage an Azure virtual machine from your laptop at home, a tablet at a coffee shop, or your phone on the bus—all through the same Azure Portal URL.

Definition: The provider's computing resources are pooled to serve multiple consumers using a multi-tenant model. Physical and virtual resources are dynamically assigned and reassigned according to demand. The consumer generally has no control or knowledge over the exact location of the resources.

Example: When you launch a VM on Google Cloud, your VM might run on hardware in Iowa today and Oregon tomorrow—you don't choose the physical server, and you share that hardware with other customers (securely isolated by the hypervisor).

Connection to your labs: Remember how a Type 1 hypervisor runs multiple VMs on one physical host? Cloud providers do this at enormous scale—thousands of hosts, millions of VMs.

Definition: Resources can be elastically provisioned and released—in some cases automatically—to scale rapidly with demand. To the consumer, the available resources often appear unlimited.

Example: An e-commerce site auto-scales from 2 web servers to 50 during a Black Friday sale, then scales back down to 2 by Saturday morning—paying only for what was used.

Definition: Cloud systems automatically control and optimize resource use by leveraging a metering capability. Resource usage is monitored, controlled, and reported, providing transparency for both the provider and consumer.

Example: AWS bills you per second of EC2 runtime, per GB of S3 storage, and per million API requests. You can view real-time cost dashboards and set billing alerts—much like monitoring your electricity usage.

Cloud Deployment Models

Not all clouds are the same. NIST defines four deployment models that describe who owns the infrastructure and who can use it.

Deployment Model Spectrum

From most controlled to most shared:

On-Premises You own everything
Private Cloud Single organization
Community Cloud Shared interest group
Hybrid Cloud Mix of private + public
Public Cloud Open to all
Comparison of cloud deployment models
ModelDescriptionExamplesBest For
Public Infrastructure owned by a cloud provider and shared among many tenants over the internet. AWS, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud Platform (GCP) Startups, variable workloads, rapid scaling
Private Infrastructure operated solely for a single organization, either on-premises or hosted by a third party. VMware vSphere private cloud, OpenStack, Azure Stack HCI Regulated industries (healthcare, finance), sensitive data
Hybrid Combination of two or more deployment models (typically private + public) bound together by standardized technology that enables data and application portability. Azure Arc, AWS Outposts, Google Anthos Organizations needing flexibility—keep sensitive data private, burst to public for peak demand
Community Shared infrastructure for a specific community of organizations with common concerns (security, compliance, jurisdiction). Government community clouds (GovCloud), healthcare data exchanges Government agencies, research consortiums, healthcare networks
💡 Key Takeaway

The deployment model answers the question: "Who owns the infrastructure and who is allowed to use it?" The service model (next sections) answers: "What level of the technology stack does the provider manage for you?"

Cloud Service Models: IaaS, PaaS, SaaS

Cloud services are categorized by how much of the technology stack the provider manages versus how much the customer manages. Use the tabs below to explore each model.

Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS)

Definition: The provider supplies virtualized computing resources—servers (VMs), storage, and networking—over the internet. The customer installs and manages the operating system, middleware, runtime, applications, and data.

🔗 Connection to Your Labs

IaaS is essentially what you have been doing in Modules 13.1–13.3, but the hypervisor is in the provider's data center instead of on your lab machine. When you create a VM in VirtualBox, you choose CPU, RAM, disk, and networking—IaaS customers do the same thing through a cloud console.

What the provider manages: Physical data centers, servers, hypervisors, networking hardware, storage hardware.

What you manage: Operating system, patches, middleware, runtime, applications, data, access control.

Real-World IaaS Examples

  • Amazon EC2 – Launch Linux or Windows VMs in seconds, choose instance types by CPU/RAM
  • Microsoft Azure Virtual Machines – Spin up Windows Server or Linux VMs with integrated Active Directory
  • Google Compute Engine – Custom machine types, per-second billing
  • DigitalOcean Droplets – Developer-friendly VMs with simple pricing

Typical IaaS Use Cases

  • Hosting web servers, database servers, or application servers
  • Development and test environments
  • Disaster recovery and backup infrastructure
  • High-performance computing (HPC) workloads

Platform as a Service (PaaS)

Definition: The provider supplies a complete development and deployment platform—including the OS, middleware, runtime, and development tools. The customer only manages the application code and data.

What the provider manages: Everything in IaaS plus the operating system, middleware, and runtime environment.

What you manage: Your application code and your data.

Real-World PaaS Examples

  • Microsoft Azure App Service – Deploy web apps in .NET, Java, Python, Node.js without managing VMs
  • Google App Engine – Fully managed platform that auto-scales your application
  • Heroku – Popular with startups for rapid deployment of web applications
  • AWS Elastic Beanstalk – Upload your code; AWS handles deployment, scaling, and patching

Typical PaaS Use Cases

  • Web application development and hosting
  • API development and management
  • Database as a Service (e.g., Azure SQL Database, Amazon RDS)
  • Rapid prototyping and CI/CD pipelines
💡 Developer Perspective

PaaS lets developers focus on writing code instead of patching operating systems. Think of it as renting a fully equipped kitchen—you bring the recipe (your code) and ingredients (your data), and the kitchen (platform) is maintained for you.

Software as a Service (SaaS)

Definition: The provider delivers a complete, ready-to-use application over the internet. The customer simply uses the software—typically through a web browser—and manages only their own data and user-level settings.

What the provider manages: Everything—hardware, OS, middleware, runtime, and the application itself.

What you manage: Your data and user-level configuration (accounts, preferences).

Real-World SaaS Examples

  • Microsoft 365 – Email (Outlook), documents (Word, Excel), collaboration (Teams)
  • Google Workspace – Gmail, Google Docs, Google Drive
  • Salesforce – Customer relationship management (CRM)
  • Splunk Cloud – The cloud-hosted version of the Splunk platform you are deploying in your lab
  • Canvas LMS – The learning management system you use every day in this course!

Typical SaaS Use Cases

  • Email and productivity suites
  • Customer relationship management (CRM)
  • Enterprise resource planning (ERP)
  • Collaboration and communication tools
⚠️ Important

Even though SaaS means the provider manages the application, the customer is still responsible for managing user access, protecting their data, and configuring application-level security settings. This is part of the shared responsibility model (covered in Section 8).

Cloud Cost Management

One of the biggest shifts from on-premises to cloud is how you pay for resources. Instead of large up-front capital expenditures (CapEx) for hardware, cloud uses an operational expenditure (OpEx) model.

Common Cloud Pricing Models

Comparison of cloud pricing models
ModelHow It WorksBest ForExample
Pay-As-You-Go
(On-Demand)		 Pay by the second, minute, or hour for resources consumed. No commitment. Variable workloads, testing, short-term projects AWS EC2 On-Demand: ~$0.023/hr for a small Linux instance
Reserved Instances Commit to 1- or 3-year terms in exchange for significant discounts (up to 72% off on-demand). Steady-state workloads, production servers that run 24/7 Azure Reserved VM Instances: pay upfront for predictable savings
Spot / Preemptible Bid on unused provider capacity at steep discounts (up to 90% off). Provider can reclaim resources with short notice. Fault-tolerant batch processing, data analysis, rendering AWS Spot Instances, GCP Preemptible VMs
⚠️ Cloud Cost Pitfall

It is easy to accidentally leave cloud resources running and rack up unexpected charges. Best practices include:

  • Set billing alerts to notify you when spending exceeds a threshold
  • Use auto-shutdown schedules for development/test VMs
  • Right-size resources—don't provision more CPU/RAM than you need
  • Review cost dashboards regularly (AWS Cost Explorer, Azure Cost Management)
🔗 CapEx vs. OpEx

CapEx (Capital Expenditure): Buying physical servers—large upfront cost, depreciates over time. This is the on-premises model.

OpEx (Operational Expenditure): Paying monthly for cloud services—no upfront cost, pay for what you use. This is the cloud model.

Many organizations use a hybrid approach: run baseline workloads on owned hardware (CapEx) and burst to the cloud (OpEx) for peak demand.

The Shared Responsibility Model

Cloud security is not all-or-nothing. The shared responsibility model defines which security tasks the cloud provider handles and which the customer must handle. The division shifts depending on the service model.

📖 Core Principle

Security of the cloud = Provider's responsibility (physical security, hypervisor, network infrastructure).

Security in the cloud = Customer's responsibility (data, access control, application configuration).

Responsibility by Service Model

Security AreaIaaSPaaSSaaS
Data classification & encryptionCustomerCustomerCustomer
Identity & access managementCustomerCustomerCustomer
Application-level securityCustomerCustomerProvider
OS patching & hardeningCustomerProviderProvider
Network controls (firewalls, ACLs)CustomerSharedProvider

⚠️ Common Misconception

"We moved to the cloud, so security is the provider's problem now." This is false. Even with SaaS, the customer is responsible for managing user accounts, configuring access controls, and protecting their data. A misconfigured sharing setting in Google Workspace can expose sensitive documents—and that's the customer's responsibility, not Google's.

Lift-and-Shift: The Migration Starting Point

Lift-and-shift (also called "rehosting") is the simplest cloud migration strategy: take an existing on-premises VM and move it to a cloud IaaS provider with minimal changes. It's not the most optimized approach, but it's often the first step organizations take.

  • Step 1: Export the on-premises VM as an image (OVA/VHD)
  • Step 2: Upload the image to the cloud provider
  • Step 3: Launch it as a cloud VM instance
  • Step 4: Reconfigure networking and DNS to point to the new location

After lift-and-shift, organizations often refactor applications to take advantage of PaaS services (databases, message queues) and eventually re-architect for cloud-native patterns (microservices, containers, serverless).

🌉 Career Relevance

The skills you are building in CSC 152—Linux/Windows server administration, networking, storage, directory services, security, and now virtualization—are the foundation for cloud roles such as:

  • Cloud Administrator – Manages cloud VMs, networks, and storage
  • Cloud Engineer – Designs and builds cloud infrastructure
  • DevOps Engineer – Automates deployment and monitoring pipelines
  • Site Reliability Engineer (SRE) – Ensures availability and performance

Relevant certifications include CompTIA Cloud+, AWS Cloud Practitioner, Azure Fundamentals (AZ-900), and Google Cloud Digital Leader.

Scenario Activity: Classify the Cloud Solution

Read each business scenario and select the best service model and deployment model. Click your answer for immediate feedback.

Scenario 1: Web Startup

A three-person startup wants to deploy a Python web application. They don't want to manage servers or operating systems—they just want to push their code and have it run. They need the service to auto-scale during traffic spikes. Budget is tight, and they want to pay only for what they use.

Which service model is the best fit?

Deployment model: Public cloud—they need broad access, elastic scaling, and minimal upfront cost.

Scenario 2: Hospital System

A hospital network needs to run Windows Server VMs hosting their electronic health records (EHR) system. They require full control over the OS for compliance auditing, but they want to eliminate the cost of maintaining their own data center hardware. Patient data must stay within the United States.

Which service model is the best fit?

Deployment model: Private or hybrid cloud—regulated healthcare data may require a private cloud or a government/healthcare-compliant region of a public cloud.

Scenario 3: School District Email

A school district with 2,000 staff members needs email, document collaboration, and video conferencing. They have no IT staff to manage servers. They want a solution that works immediately with just a web browser.

Which service model is the best fit?

Deployment model: Public cloud—broadly accessible, no infrastructure to manage, cost-effective for education.
